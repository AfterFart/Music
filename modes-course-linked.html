§ 01 — Foundation

Why Everyone Gets Modes Wrong

The standard explanation goes like this: "Dorian starts on the 2nd degree of the major scale. Phrygian starts on the 3rd…" And then you think — so what? They're all just the same notes with a different starting point. Why would I ever use that?

That explanation is technically correct and practically useless. It tells you what a mode is, but not why it sounds different, or when to use it.

The Key Insight

A mode is not about starting on a different note. A mode is about which chord you're playing over. The same note pattern takes on completely different emotional colour depending on what bass note and chord is underneath it. The relationship between the notes and the root creates the flavour.

Think of it this way: the word "fire" means something different in "I love fire" versus "You're fired" versus "Fire the cannons." Same word, completely different meaning because of context. Modes are notes whose meaning changes based on harmonic context.

A Quick Thought Experiment

Play these notes over a C major chord: C D E F G A B. You hear C major (Ionian). Now keep playing the exact same notes, but now there's a D minor chord underneath. Suddenly D is the tonal centre. The F sounds lower than expected. The C sounds like it wants to resolve somewhere. You're hearing D Dorian — same notes, different world.

Practical Rule

Before anything else: a mode's sound only exists in relation to a drone or chord on its root. Without that anchor, you're just playing a major scale from a different note. The chord underneath is what makes it a mode.

§ 02 — The Real Secret

How to Actually Think About Modes

Forget which degree they start on. Think instead about how they differ from major or minor. Every mode is a flavour variation on one of two basic tastes: major (bright) or minor (dark). Learning the modification is all you need.

Mode Based On Key Modification Emotional Colour
Ionian Major Pure major — no change Happy, resolved, bright
Dorian Minor Minor with a raised 6th Dark but cool, soulful, funky
Phrygian Minor Minor with a ♭2 — very exotic Dark, Spanish, menacing
Lydian Major Major with a #4 — dreamlike Floating, cinematic, ethereal
Mixolydian Major Major with a ♭7 — bluesy Rock, blues, dominant energy
Aeolian Minor Pure natural minor — no change Sad, classic, melancholic
Locrian Minor Minor with ♭2 AND ♭5 — unstable Tense, dissonant, unstable
The Two Families

Major family (bright 3rd): Ionian, Lydian, Mixolydian.
Minor family (dark 3rd): Dorian, Phrygian, Aeolian, Locrian.
When you're over a major chord, you pick from the major family. Over a minor chord, you pick from the minor family. Simple as that.

§ 04 — Colour Notes

The Note That Defines the Mode

Each mode has one or two characteristic notes — the intervals that make it unmistakably itself. These are the notes you should emphasise, bend, and resolve to when you want the mode to speak. If you land on these notes, you've arrived.

Practice Tip

When learning each mode, start every lick on the characteristic note, bend it, and resolve to the root. That 3-note phrase will immediately sound like the mode. Forget scales for a moment — just find that note and milk it.

§ 06 — Mode Shifting

Shifting Modes in a Solo

This is the big payoff. A mode shift happens when the chord underneath you changes, and you change your melodic colour to match. You're not "changing scales" — you're changing your perspective on the harmonic situation.

Strategy 1 — Follow the Chord

The most natural approach: when a minor chord appears, think minor-mode family. When a major chord appears, think major-mode family. The notes often overlap completely, but your emphasis and the characteristic note you lean on changes.

Example Solo: ii–V–I in C major

Over the chord progression Dm7 → G7 → Cmaj7, you shift mode with every chord. The notes barely change — but the flavour transforms completely.

Strategy 2 — The Modal Vamp (One Chord)

On a static one-chord vamp, you control what mode colour the listener hears by choosing which notes to emphasise. Over a D minor vamp, you can play D Aeolian for sadness, D Dorian for groove, or D Phrygian for menace — all with the same underlying notes.

Over Dm — Three Colours

Version 1 · D Aeolian · "Classic sad minor"
Emphasise the B♭ (minor 6th). Avoid the B natural.

The Key Realisation

Notice in Strategy 2 above: the only notes that changed between Aeolian and Dorian are a single semitone (B♭ vs B natural). That's all it takes to completely shift the emotional flavour. Modes are not about learning seven scales — they're about understanding how one or two pivotal notes can transform the feeling of your solo.

How to Practice Mode Shifting

Use this three-step drill. It will feel mechanical at first, then suddenly click.

The "One Note Difference" Drill
Daily Practice

Step 1: Set up a looper or backing track with a simple Dm vamp.

Step 2: Solo freely using D Aeolian for 4 bars. Really lean on the B♭.

Step 3: Switch to D Dorian — change only the B♭ to B natural. Everything else stays the same. Do this consciously for 4 bars.

Step 4: Now switch freely between them mid-phrase. You're now choosing your colour, not just running scales.

Final Thought

Modes become real when you hear the difference, not when you memorise the formula. Spend one week doing nothing but D Dorian vs D Aeolian over a drone note. Once you can hear and feel that single note change, the entire system opens up. The other modes are just more variations of the same idea.
