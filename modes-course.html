Modal Interchange — Ionian ↔ Lydian

When Fmaj7#11 arrives, your melody can briefly borrow B♮ (#4 of F Lydian) as its peak note — one semitone above the diatonic F. That single chromatic note creates the "floating lift" of film scores before Ionian resolves it back to E.

Pro Technique — Legato Economy

Pick only the first note per string; slur everything after. Your right hand barely moves. The result is a fluid, vocal line that alternate-picked scale runs simply cannot match.

Modal Interchange — Lydian → Ionian (the "landing")

Build tension with B♮ (#4), then resolve by dropping to B♭ (Ionian's natural 4th). That one-semitone fall is the "landing" — dream to reality. Film composers use it at every emotional climax.

Sweep Technique

One continuous downstroke across all strings — mute each note with the fretting hand immediately after it sounds. Never let notes blur together. The result is a harp-like cascade, not a strummed chord. Keep the pick angle shallow.

The One-Note Revelation

Aeolian → Dorian is B♭ → B♮: one semitone, the difference between melancholic and soulful. Aeolian → Phrygian is E♮ → E♭: one semitone, the difference between classic minor and menacing. Modes are not seven different scales. They are the same notes with one or two pivots that change everything.

Exercise 10 — Modal Interchange
Advanced Harmony Concept
Borrowing Across Parallel Modes
Modal Interchange Borrowed Chords Chromatic Targets
★★★★★

Borrowing chords from parallel modes (same root, different scale) creates unexpected emotional turns that feel right even though they break diatonic rules. The solo must acknowledge borrowed chords by targeting the "foreign" accidental on the downbeat.

Common Borrowed Chords in C Major

FROM C AEOLIAN
♭VII · B♭ major
C → B♭ → F → C. "Sweet Child O' Mine", "Hey Jude" outro. Target: B♭ on the downbeat.
FROM C PHRYGIAN
♭II · D♭ major
The Neapolitan. Dramatic surprise. Target: D♭ on arrival.
FROM C LYDIAN
#IV · Fmaj7#11
The "lift". C → Fmaj7#11 → G → C. Beatles, Williams. Target: B♮ (#4).

Soloing Over Interchange

When a borrowed chord arrives, your melody must land on the accidental that makes it foreign — on the downbeat. If you ignore it, the borrowed chord sounds like a mistake. If you target it, it sounds inevitable.

The Rule in Practice

C major → B♭: land on B♭ on beat 1.
B♭ → back to C: return to B♮ on beat 1.
That one semitone oscillation carries the entire emotional arc.

The Advanced Mindset

At the expert level you stop asking "which mode?" and ask: "what colour does this moment need?" Modal interchange is how you create that colour deliberately. The borrowed chord is a door — your melody decides whether to walk through it, peek through it, or lean against it. All three are valid. The point is you are choosing, not reacting.
